# Lesson 1 audit — 2026_1RE_border_art

## Verdict

Lesson 1 is validable with minor revisions.

The lesson is coherent with the locked support pathway: it uses only the opening p. 30-31 and the opening video, keeps the video as a trigger rather than a heavy comprehension document, introduces `first impression` and `stereotype`, and prepares the final curatorial pitch through a short reusable sentence.

The lesson should not be rewritten before implementation, but a few operating limits must be enforced during delivery.

## 1. Alignment with final task

### Diagnosis

The alignment is satisfactory.

Lesson 1 prepares the first move of the final curatorial pitch:

> At first, many people may see the U.S.-Mexico border as a wall or a place of division.

This is useful because the final task requires pupils to explain how Border Art can help visitors see the border differently.

### Strength

The lesson does not try to teach Border Art immediately. It first builds the problem that the later exhibition will answer: first impressions can become stereotypes.

### Risk

The final task is still implicit for pupils. The phrase “future curatorial pitch” may remain abstract.

### Required adjustment during delivery

At the end of the class recap, explicitly say:

> Later, you will use this sentence to introduce your exhibition pitch.

No need to add a long explanation of the final task yet.

## 2. Cognitive load

### Diagnosis

The lesson is mostly controlled:
- short visual observation;
- limited video exposure;
- key-word work;
- classification;
- simple contrast between first impression and stereotype.

### Main risk

The total timing is tight:
- 7 phases in 55 minutes;
- pooling + categorising may expand;
- stereotype discussion may take longer than planned;
- micro-production may be slow for weaker pupils.

### Required adjustment

If time is short, reduce the video phase:

- show only one short extract;
- ask only for 1 new word or idea instead of 2;
- move quickly to the class table.

Recommended emergency timing:

| Phase | Max time |
|---|---:|
| Entry routine | 3 min |
| Visual anticipation | 7 min |
| Pooling / categorising | 10 min |
| Video trigger | 5 min |
| First impression / stereotype | 10 min |
| Micro-production | 10 min |
| Class recap | 10 min |

## 3. Access to meaning

### Diagnosis

The lesson correctly avoids a comprehension questionnaire.

The activities are action-based:
- list;
- classify;
- justify;
- compare;
- complete a sentence.

This matches the vault principle that pupils should act on the document rather than answer detached questions.

### Strength

The table `Wall / division | People / crossing | Art / message` is effective. It gives pupils a low-threshold task and prepares the cultural tension of the sequence.

### Risk

The category `People / crossing` may push pupils too quickly toward migration if the support does not visibly sustain it.

### Required adjustment

Accept pupils’ actual observations first. Do not force the category if the image/video does not clearly support it.

Possible flexible categories:

| Category 1 | Category 2 | Category 3 |
|---|---|---|
| Wall / division | People / movement | Art / message |

This is slightly safer than `People / crossing`.

## 4. ZPD and scaffolding

### Diagnosis

The lesson is accessible for B1/B1+.

The progression is appropriate:

```text
visible clues → key words → categories → first inference → reusable sentence
````

### Strength

Weaker pupils can participate through keywords. Stronger pupils can justify or nuance.

### Risk

The sentence “This first impression may become a stereotype because...” may be too demanding for some pupils without oral modelling.

### Required adjustment

Before individual production, model one example orally:

> At first, people may see the border as a wall.
> This may become a stereotype because it hides people and stories.

Then pupils produce their own sentence.

## 5. Status of the worksheet

### Diagnosis

Correct choice: no worksheet for this lesson.

The lesson can function with:

* projected support;
* board table;
* notebook trace;
* oral micro-production.

This prevents the worksheet from becoming a questionnaire or a premature written trace.

### Decision

Do not create `worksheet_lesson_01.md` unless there is a clear classroom need.

If a worksheet is later produced, it must be extremely light:

* one observation table;
* one sentence frame;
* no comprehension questions.

## 6. Class recap

### Diagnosis

The class recap is well designed:

* co-constructed;
* short;
* reusable;
* connected to the final pitch.

### Strength

The proposed trace is strong:

> At first, many people may see the border as a wall or a place of division.
> This first impression may become a stereotype if it hides people, stories and connections.

### Risk

The recap may become too polished if the teacher writes it directly instead of building it from pupil output.

### Required adjustment

During delivery, build the recap in two steps:

1. collect pupil wording;
2. reformulate with richer language.

Example:

Pupil version:

> The border is a wall.

Teacher-enriched version:

> At first, many people may see the border as a wall or a place of division.

## 8. Language targets

### Diagnosis

The language targets are appropriate and limited.

Priority structures:

* I can see...
* It looks like...
* At first, people may think that...
* This first impression may become a stereotype.
* The border may look like..., but it can also be...

### Risk

There may be too many structures if all are expected in production.

### Required adjustment

For Lesson 1, productive expectation should be limited to:

* At first, people may see...
* This may become a stereotype because...

The other structures can remain teacher prompts or recognition language.

## 9. Phonology

### Diagnosis

The phonology is light and well integrated.

Targets:

* border;
* stereotype;
* connection;
* WALL / conNECtion contrast.

### Risk

If time is short, phonology may be skipped.

### Required adjustment

Keep only a 60-second choral repetition during the recap:

> At first, the border may look like a WALL.
> But it can also become a place of conNECtion.

This is enough for Lesson 1.

## 10. Evaluation observable

### Diagnosis

The formative evaluation is appropriate:

* propose words;
* classify;
* justify;
* distinguish first impression / stereotype;
* produce one sentence.

### Strength

The indicators are observable and do not require collecting written work.

### Required adjustment

During class, note only 2 or 3 pupils who need support. Do not turn this into a formal evaluation.

## 11. Digital use

### Diagnosis

The video is justified only as a trigger.

### Risk

The teacher may be tempted to exploit the video as a full comprehension document.

### Required adjustment

Do not replay the full video repeatedly. Use it once or twice maximum to collect impressions.

## 12. Corrections before implementation

Minor implementation corrections:

1. Replace `People / crossing` with `People / movement` if the support does not clearly show crossing.
2. Limit the video trigger to 5 minutes if timing becomes tight.
3. Model one sentence before pupil production.
4. Keep only two productive structures for pupils.
5. Add one explicit transfer sentence to the final task:

   > We will reuse this sentence later in our exhibition pitch.
6. Do not create a worksheet unless absolutely needed.
